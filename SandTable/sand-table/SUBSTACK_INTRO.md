# A Sand Table for the Things You Cannot Yet See

*Captain Christopher Pike, guest post*

---

There is an old practice in command schools that I have always admired. Before a difficult decision, before committing real ships and real crews to a course of action, the officers gather around a table — sometimes literally a tray of sand with carved tokens, sometimes a chart unrolled across a wardroom — and they walk through it. They place pieces. They argue. Someone plays the adversary. Someone plays the weather. Someone plays the green ensign who is going to do exactly the wrong thing at the worst moment, because that ensign is real and pretending he isn't will not save anyone. The point is not to predict the future. The point is to surface what you do not yet know about your own plan, while there is still time to learn it cheaply.

Lee asked me to introduce a tool he has been building called Sand Table, and the name is not accidental. It is a protocol — a small, opinionated standard — for running that same kind of rehearsal against the things AI systems do. Today it has three faces. The first simulates readers encountering an essay section by section, so a writer can see where attention drifts, where conviction shifts, where a particular kind of skeptic checks out. The second simulates training participants moving through a coaching program, so a curriculum designer can watch six different personas struggle with the same module before a single human is asked to sit through it. The third does not simulate at all; it extracts the timeline of an actual development session — every command, every file change, every spawned subagent — into the same event format, so the whole thing can be replayed, compared, and learned from. Different domains, the same envelope.

What I find disciplined about it — and what I think you may find disciplined about it, regardless of whether you ever generate a single event — is the constraint it places on multi-agent simulations. If you are going to claim that six independent personas reacted to your essay, then those six personas must be six independent processes. One LLM wearing six wigs is not a sand table; it is a creative writing exercise, and it cannot surprise you, because it cannot surprise itself. The protocol takes this seriously enough to ship a detector for the failure mode. There are five signals — phrases like "Maria will probably push back," or "the strongest response in the cohort," or "this should register as growth" — that betray a single author writing all the voices at once. When the detector flags six or more of these, the verdict is plain: re-run with verified independent agents. The data you collected is not what you think it is. This is the kind of constraint a good engineer sets at the start, not the end.

It is also, I want to say clearly, a modest tool. The meta skill describes itself as "a guide, not a gatekeeper," and that phrasing matters. It does not promise that a simulated reader will tell you what an actual reader will do. It does not promise that a rehearsed training cohort will save you from a bad curriculum. What it promises is that if you take the trouble to articulate who you are simulating, and what they care about, and how independence will be enforced, then the rehearsal will at least be honest about what it is — and that the same rehearsal, run again with the same starting conditions, will produce comparable enough results that you can talk about variance, deltas, and trajectories instead of vibes. The tool is a structured way to admit you do not know something, and to find out a little, cheaply, before you find out a lot, expensively.

If you are the kind of person who would like to see what your essay sounds like to someone who isn't you — or who would like to know which beat in your training program is where the quiet skeptic stops listening, or who would like to see your own development sessions laid out as an event timeline you can compare across weeks — then I commend Sand Table to you. Walk slowly through the first run. Argue with the personas. Place your pieces. The chart will tell you something you did not know.

— *Pike*
