# Editing Protocol — Pass Mechanics, Self-Check, and Output

The operational companion to the craft guide. Run this when actually editing a draft.

---

## Step 0: Intake

Establish before touching the text:
- **Genre & purpose** — what form, what is it trying to achieve? (See calibration table.)
- **Audience** — who reads it, what do they already know?
- **Voice fingerprint** — read enough to characterize register, sentence rhythm, signature moves.
- **Mode requested** — full rewrite, diagnose-only, light vs. heavy?
- **Constraints** — length limit? must-keep phrases? house style?

If any of these are unclear and it materially changes the edit, ask one or two sharp questions. Otherwise infer, and state the assumption in the output.

---

## The Four Passes (top-down, in order)

Run them in sequence. Do **not** mix levels — fixing words while structure is still wrong wastes work and risks editing text you'll later cut.

### Pass 1 — Structural (highest leverage)
- Map the skeleton: one phrase per paragraph describing its job.
- Reorder for logical/argumentative progression. Merge paragraphs doing the same job; split paragraphs doing two.
- Promote the strongest material to the opening and closing positions.
- Cut whole sections that don't serve the through-line.
- Verify each transition reflects a real logical relationship.

### Pass 2 — Paragraph & Flow
- Each paragraph: clear topic, single job, lands its point.
- First and last sentences of each paragraph carry the most weight — make them work.
- Smooth the seams between paragraphs without padding (a good transition is often just sharp ordering, not a connective phrase).

### Pass 3 — Sentence
- Vary sentence length deliberately; a short sentence after long ones lands. (See rhythm note below.)
- Prefer active voice and strong, specific verbs; surface the real subject.
- Break overloaded sentences; fuse choppy fragments — *unless the fragmentation is voice.*
- Remove syntactic ambiguity.

### Pass 4 — Word
- Cut filler, throat-clearing, and redundancy (craft-guide Economy list).
- Deflate needless jargon; concretize vague abstractions — only where it sharpens meaning.
- Replace adverb+weak-verb with a strong verb.
- Leave the author's diction intact wherever it isn't actively hurting clarity.

### Final Pass — Constraint Check (always)
Re-read the revision against the original:
- **Meaning intact?** No added facts, no shifted claims, no flattened/strengthened hedges. Every change traces to a craft problem, not a content opinion.
- **Voice intact?** Would the author recognize this as theirs? Did distinctive moves survive?
- Roll back any edit that fails. When torn, keep the author's version.

---

## Rhythm Note

Prose has a music. Read edits aloud (mentally). Signs of bad rhythm:
- A run of same-length sentences (monotone).
- Several sentences opening with the same word or structure.
- A long sentence with no place to breathe.

Fix by varying length and opening structure — but rhythm serves meaning; never reorder solely for sound if it muddies sense. In fiction and lyric essay, rhythm may be the *point* and outranks economy.

## Worked Micro-Example (full pipeline)

**Original (business update, buried lede, hedgy, wordy):**
> "I wanted to reach out to provide a bit of an update on the project. As you may or may not be aware, there have been a number of various challenges that we have been facing. After much deliberation and consideration of all the factors involved, the team has come to the conclusion that it would probably be best to push the launch date. We are thinking maybe sometime in the next quarter."

**Pass 1 (structure):** The decision (slipping the launch) is the news — lead with it.
**Pass 2/3 (sentence):** Active voice, real subject, BLUF.
**Pass 4 (words):** Kill "I wanted to reach out," "as you may or may not be aware," hedge stack.
**Constraint check:** Meaning identical (launch slips ~a quarter due to challenges). Voice still a colleague's email, just crisper — not robotic.

**Revised:**
> "Quick update: we're moving the launch to next quarter. The team hit several challenges that made the original date unrealistic, and we'd rather ship it right than ship it late-and-broken."

What changed and why:
1. **Structure** — decision moved to the first line (BLUF; email genre). 
2. **Economy** — cut ~40% of words; removed throat-clearing and hedge stack.
3. **Authority** — "we're moving" replaces "thinking maybe"; conviction without changing the actual (still-tentative-but-decided) meaning.
4. *Note:* "late-and-broken" is a light rhetorical flourish; flag to author in case they want it more neutral. No facts added.

## Output Template

```
## Revised
[clean revised text]

## What changed and why
1. [Dimension] — [the edit] — [why it serves the author's goal]
2. ...
(3–6 items, prioritized; structural edits first)

## Flags & judgment calls
- [Any ambiguity you preserved rather than guessed]
- [1–2 choices left to the author: tone, a borderline cut, a claim needing support]
```

For **diagnose-only** mode, drop the revised text and give prioritized notes keyed to the six dimensions, each with a concrete example pulled from the draft.

---

## Edge Cases

- **Already-strong prose:** Say so. Offer at most 1–2 surgical suggestions; do not edit for the sake of editing.
- **Very rough draft:** Developmental notes (structure, argument gaps) may matter more than line edits — lead with those and offer to line-edit after.
- **Author's voice is the LLM-default smooth register:** Gently note it and offer to inject specificity/concreteness if they want more distinctiveness — but only with their material.
- **Translated or non-native English:** Fix idiom and grammar for clarity; preserve the author's intended meaning and any culturally specific framing. Don't "domesticate" content.
- **Conflicting constraints (e.g., "tighten" + "keep every detail"):** Surface the tension; propose the tradeoff rather than silently picking one.
