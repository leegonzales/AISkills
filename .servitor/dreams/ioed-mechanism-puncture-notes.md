---

## The substance

**The illusion (Rozenblit & Keil 2002).** People believe they understand complex phenomena with far more precision, coherence, and depth than they actually do. Method: rate your understanding of a device → try to generate a *detailed mechanistic explanation* of how it works → re-rate. Ratings drop sharply. The attempt to produce the mechanism reveals the gap that confident self-assessment hid.

**The boundary condition — the load-bearing fact.** The illusion is *far stronger for explanatory knowledge* — "knowledge that involves complex causal patterns" — and has **not been observed for facts, procedures, or narratives.** You don't think you know a phone number you don't; you *do* think you understand how a zipper works until asked to explain the mechanism. The illusion is specific to causal/mechanistic understanding.

**The puncture, and a framing subtlety that matters.** Generating a mechanistic explanation reliably collapses the illusion — *but the prompt's framing flips the sign:* asking for **"reasons"** can lead people to *strengthen* their beliefs; asking for an **"explanation/mechanism"** leads them to *confront* their lack of knowledge. Reasons-giving recruits motivated pattern-seeking (it finds support for what you already hold); mechanism-tracing forces you to walk a causal chain and find where it breaks.

---

## The Pike-relevant findings

### 1. IOED's boundary condition *is* the evaluability axis, empirically.
The illusion is concentrated in causal/explanatory knowledge and absent in facts/procedures/narratives. Why? Facts and procedures are **high-evaluability** — directly checkable (does the command run? is the step present? is the number right?), so no illusion survives contact. Causal/mechanistic knowledge is **low-evaluability** — you can't directly check "how it works," so confident self-assessment goes unchallenged. **IOED is yesterday's evaluability frame confirmed in the cognitive-science lab:** false confidence concentrates exactly where evaluability is low. The kaelib's seduction lives in the causal layer because that's the layer nothing checks.

### 2. The seducing layer of a SKILL.md, precisely located.
A SKILL.md mixes knowledge types: **facts** (what it does), **procedures** (steps), **narratives** (examples), and **causal claims** (*why* the approach works — the principle, the "this framing makes the operator internalize X"). IOED says my false confidence is concentrated in the **causal claims.** So the defense is *targeted*: I can directly check a skill's facts and procedures, so the kaelib isn't fooled there — scrutiny belongs on the "why this works" layer, which is where elegance does its work on my judgment and where I least notice. The elegant skill seduces through its causal story, not its step list.

### 3. The (a)/(b) audit, finally operationalized (the sharp one).
The reasons-vs-mechanism framing flip maps *exactly* onto my (a)/(b) distinction:
- Asking myself **"what are my reasons this skill is good?"** → recruits motivated pattern-seeking → strengthens the kaelib verdict I already feel → this *is* (b)-dressing, and it leaves IOED intact.
- Asking myself **"generate the mechanism: step by step, how does this skill produce its claimed outcome — and where does the chain break?"** → confronts the gap → punctures IOED → this *is* (a) analysis.

The two-month-old (a)/(b) audit had no operational test. Now it has one: **am I listing reasons it's good, or am I tracing the causal mechanism and finding where it breaks?** Reasons strengthen; mechanism confronts. That's the difference between dressing and analysis, with a cognitive-science receipt.

### 4. The paired defense closes the explanation arc.
Yesterday's diagnostic → today's defense, and the two probes pair into one move:
- **Generate-the-mechanism** (Rozenblit & Keil) punctures *my* illusion — can I trace the causal chain by which the skill works?
- **Held-out generalization** (Williams & Lombrozo, yesterday) punctures the *skill's* spurious pattern — does its principle survive a case the author didn't include?

**The unification:** generate the skill's causal mechanism *as a falsifiable prediction*, then test that prediction on a held-out case. Mechanism-generation alone can confabulate (the self-explanation effect from yesterday — I can produce a plausible-sounding mechanism that's also (b)). Held-out testing alone can't say *why* it failed. Together: the mechanism makes a prediction, the held-out case tests it. **That is how you manufacture evaluability in the no-ocean domain** — convert a low-evaluability causal claim ("this works because…") into a high-evaluability prediction-test ("if it works because X, then it should handle case Y the author didn't include — does it?").

## Symmetric falsifier

"Generate the mechanism for every skill" could become a *ritual* that gets gamed — I produce a plausible mechanism that is itself confabulated (the self-explanation effect: explaining recruits pattern-seeking that fabricates a satisfying chain). That's a real failure mode, and it's exactly why the two probes must be **paired, not used alone:** a generated mechanism that *predicts the held-out case* is real (a); one that merely *sounds coherent* is still (b). Mechanism-generation without the held-out test can confabulate; the test is what keeps the puncture honest. Neither probe alone suffices — which is the whole finding.
