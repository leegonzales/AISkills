## The four named swells

From Marshallese ethnography (Davenport 1960, Laubenfels 1950, summarized in current sources):

- **Rilib** — "backbone." Strongest of the four. Present year-round. Generated by the northeast trade winds.
- **Kaelib** — weaker than rilib. Present year-round. Only detectable by knowledgeable persons.
- **Bungdockerik** — arises in the southwest. Present year-round. Often as strong as rilib in southern islands.
- **Bundockeing** — weakest. Mainly felt in northern islands.

The naming encodes a causal taxonomy: each swell corresponds to a distinct wind or storm system, and the geographic gradient (where each is strongest) reveals its source region.

## What Genz et al. (2009) measured

Genz, Aucan, Merrifield, Finney, Joel, and Kelen ran the modern oceanographic project (Kapeel in Meto / Indigenous Knowledge of the Ocean) that brought wave-buoy measurements, satellite imagery, and SWAN wave-model simulations alongside ethnographic fieldwork with surviving navigators including Captain Korent Joel.

Their swell climatology (from ECMWF 40-Year Reanalysis):

- **Dominant signal year-round: easterly trade wind swell.** Direction varies seasonally — northeast in boreal winter (Nov-Mar), east, southeast in boreal summer (April-Sep). Typical wave period ~10 seconds.
- **Southwest swells:** occur Aug-Dec. Intermittent. "Limited in duration and would seem to be unreliable for navigational purposes."
- **North Pacific extratropical storms:** can produce strong irregular events, but "too strong and irregular for navigation."

This is the substrate. The Marshallese names map onto it cleanly:

| Marshallese name | Oceanographic identification | Source system | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rilib** | Easterly trade wind swell (dominant) | NE trade winds | Year-round, strongest, ~10s period |
| **Kaelib** | Likely *reflected/superposition wave field* from rilib interacting with island bathymetry (see below); or a secondary trade-wind harmonic | Same trade-wind system, but the weaker signature | Year-round, at perception floor |
| **Bungdockerik** | Southwest swell (Genz's Aug-Dec intermittent component) | Southern Hemisphere storms tracking northward; possibly counter-equatorial current effects | Strongest in southern Marshalls (closer to source) |
| **Bundockeing** | North Pacific extratropical swell | Long-period swells from N. Pacific winter storms | Strongest in northern Marshalls (closer to source) |

The geographic gradient encoded in the indigenous taxonomy matches the oceanographic source-region gradient. Bungdockerik is strongest in the south because the SW swell originates south of the archipelago. Bundockeing is strongest in the north because the NP storms originate north. The naming holds the source-system geography.

## The kaelib's special status — what Genz actually measured

The most striking finding in the Genz paper is what they measured for *reflected wave energy* upstream of islands. Captain Korent identified, by feel, a pattern of waves reflected back to sea from islands as the trade-wind swell strikes the windward shore. This pattern was detectable to him as canoe motion at distances up to **40 km from the island**.

Quote from the paper: *"Navigators identified a superposition of incident swells with reflected waves 40 km upstream of islands. These reflected waves were too weak to be detected by the wave buoy, but they are conceptualized similarly within indigenous and scientific frameworks."*

That sentence is worth sitting with.

A Datawell Directional Waverider G-4 wave buoy — a GPS-based motion sensor specifically designed to measure wave height, frequency, and direction across full directional spectra — could not detect the reflected wave energy that a trained navigator perceived through canoe motion. The wave buoy and the navigator-in-canoe were both directional motion sensors; the navigator was the higher-sensitivity instrument for that specific low-amplitude long-wavelength signal at that distance.

Genz et al. don't frame this romantically. They frame it as parallel knowledge systems: indigenous and Western science conceptualize the same phenomenon (wave reflection, superposition); only the indigenous detector reached the necessary sensitivity. The instrument-versus-body comparison is documented as empirical fact in the published paper.

**The kaelib lives in this gap.** The "weakest signal, only detectable by trained navigators" the original digest entry named is not metaphorical training-respect language. It's the actual mechanism: there are reflected long-wavelength low-amplitude signals at 20-40 km from islands that are below the detection threshold of a research-grade directional wave buoy and *at* the floor of trained-body perception. The signal exists in oceanographic reality; only the trained instrument can pick it up.

## What this clarifies for the dream-digest

The "kaelib test" entry in the Acquired Lenses section of the digest already had the principle right: *"the most informative signal is often the faintest one — only detectable by trained practitioners."* What I have now that I didn't before is the **mechanism**.

It's not just that experts notice subtle things. It's that the human-body-in-motion is a particular kind of high-sensitivity directional motion sensor for low-amplitude long-wavelength signals, and that sensor category has had no instrumental equivalent until very recently (and even then with difficulty in the field environment Captain Korent worked in). The kaelib principle is grounded in *physiological measurement reality*, not in deference to tradition.

That distinction matters because it means: when a senior reviewer says *"this skill doesn't quite work"* and can't articulate why, they may be detecting a low-amplitude long-wavelength reflected signal that the explicit-rubric instrument (the 85/100 categories) is below detection threshold for. The kaelib is operating.
