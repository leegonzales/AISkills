# Polynesian Wayfinding — Research Notes
*Dream Cycle: 2026-04-09*

## The Three Chart Types (Marshall Islands)

The Marshallese made three kinds of stick charts from coconut palm ribs and cowrie shells:

**Mattang (or wappepe):**
- Square, abstract
- Models one island's wave interactions
- Teaches principles: refraction, reflection, diffraction
- Used *only* for teaching — never on a voyage
- "The mattang is the conceptual framework underlining the wave piloting system" (Academia.edu)
- Generalizes; does not represent a specific route

**Meddo:**
- A few islands
- Models swell patterns for a specific route
- Route-specific guidance

**Rebbelib:**
- Entire island chain (or most of it)
- Strategic understanding of the whole system
- Scale: the big picture

**Critical fact:** None of these charts were taken on the voyage.

> "They're not brought on board in order to navigate; rather, the navigator makes them as a personal reference, studying them while on land and bringing that knowledge onto the sea." — JSTOR Daily

The chart is a scaffold for building understanding that then lives in the body. Once the voyage starts, the scaffold stays on shore.

## The Proprioceptive Dimension

At sea, Marshallese navigators navigated by crouching or lying prone in the bow of the canoe, feeling how the hull was being pitched and rolled by subsurface swells.

The navigator's body IS the instrument.

They tracked four named swells:
- **Rilib** — dominant, from the east, year-round
- **Kaelib** — weaker, only detectable by trained navigators
- **Bungdockerik** — a third swell
- **Bundockeing** — weakest, mainly felt in the northern islands

The kaelib is the one that tells you most — because it is faint, only those who have trained their bodies to hear it can navigate by it.

## The Dekä: Training by Scaling

The JSTOR Daily article mentions a *dekä* — a reef used as a training environment.

> "The reef models a destination atoll and the motion of the near-shore wave patterns models that of ocean swells flowing from the east and west. The magnitude of these sensations of movement at the dekä (miniscule!) are scaled up in the navigator's mind in preparation for ocean voyaging."

You learn to feel small waves in a safe environment. Then you scale that felt knowledge up to the open ocean in your mind. You practice sensing, then you practice imagining the scaling, then you do it for real.

This is a three-stage progression: concept (mattang) → scaled practice (dekä) → actual voyaging.

## The Etak Concept (Caroline Islands)

From Micronesian navigation more broadly: the *etak* is a cognitive inversion that changes the fundamental question of navigation.

In Western navigation: *I move through a fixed landscape.*
In etak: *I am stationary. A reference island moves past me.*

The navigator picks a reference island off to one side of their route — an island they cannot see, below the horizon. They track which stars that invisible island is under as the voyage progresses. As different stars pass over the reference island's position, the navigator knows they've completed another leg of the journey.

> "The island appears in the navigator's imagination to move back along the horizon, such that the navigator creates a model of the voyage that they can manipulate from their point of view." — ScienceDirect, "Wayfinding across ocean and tundra"

This inversion is not just a mnemonic trick. It's a different phenomenology. You don't ask *where am I?* You ask *what has moved past me?*

The canoe is the center of a navigational universe. The world moves; you remain.

## The Star Compass: A Mental Construct

From Nainoa Thompson (Hōkūleʻa):

> "The star compass is the basic mental construct for navigation... It is a mental construct to help you memorize what you need to know to navigate. You cannot look up at the stars and tell where you are. You only know where you are in this kind of navigation by memorizing where you sailed from."

The star compass is not a physical object. It is a structure of knowledge that exists only in the navigator's mind. The horizon is divided into 32 "houses" — positions where stars rise and set. You memorize which house each star belongs to. You track which stars are rising and setting as you sail.

But the compass tells you *direction*, not *position*. You know where you are only by integrating: direction × speed × time, from the moment you left. Constant observation. No resting.

This is dead reckoning as a living practice.

## The Transmission Problem

Navigation knowledge in the Marshall Islands was:
- Closely guarded (family of the chief, father to son)
- Passed through oral tradition, often in song
- Requiring decades of training, beginning in childhood
- Both theoretical (mattang, star compass) and embodied (dekä, actual voyaging)

The Royal Society paper on tacit knowledge transmission is relevant here:

> "That effective subset of interventions (a kernel), when placed in an embodied context, reliably activates the characteristic and flexible behaviours of an expert. The nature of tacit knowledge means that the teacher is unaware of the exact nature of practice she exemplifies."

The teacher cannot fully articulate what they know. The student cannot simply imitate the teacher. Transmission requires a structured environment — the mattang, the dekä, the squadron of canoes — that creates conditions for embodied knowledge to develop.
