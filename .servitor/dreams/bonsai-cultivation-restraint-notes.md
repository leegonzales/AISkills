## The Golden Rule

Bonsai practitioners summarize the entire discipline as: **patience and observation.** Not technique, not aesthetic theory, not species knowledge. Patience and observation. Everything else is in service to those two.

This is striking because the framing assumes the practitioner *will be tempted to act* and the discipline is to *not act* — to watch instead. The contrast: a craft framed as "skill" assumes you don't yet know enough; a craft framed as "patience and observation" assumes you know more than enough and the failure mode is intervention, not ignorance.

---

## The energy-positive principle

The technical core of bonsai timing: **a tree must be in an "energy-positive" state to absorb a cut.**

Trees store energy in spring (after new growth hardens) and fall (before winter). In early spring, stored energy is being expended pushing new growth — the tree is in *energy deficit*. Pruning at this time impacts the tree's health because the cut demands recovery resources the tree doesn't have. Pruning during energy-deficit weakens; severe pruning during deficit kills.

Pruning during energy-positive states is absorbed gracefully. The tree has the resources to redirect energy toward new patterns the cut creates.

**Same intervention; different consequences depending on the substrate's state at the time of intervention.**

## The "treading water" anti-pattern

The most important failure mode: *let the bonsai grow, then prune back exactly what it just pushed out.*

This wastes the tree's energy investments and prevents meaningful structural development. Each season cancels itself. The tree expends resources growing, the practitioner removes that growth, the tree expends more resources to push again, the cycle repeats. From outside it looks like maintenance. From the tree's perspective it's continuous depletion with no progress.

This is the pattern impatient practitioners default into. They want to keep the bonsai "looking like a bonsai" and so they cut anything that breaks the silhouette. But silhouette-maintenance and structural development are *opposite work*. To develop structure, you must let some growth go further than the silhouette before deciding what to cut and what to keep. The silhouette must be allowed to be temporarily wrong.

## Three developmental stages

Different stages of a bonsai's life require different interventions:

- **Stage 1 — Grow.** Heavy fertilization, minimal intervention. The tree needs unrestrained growth to develop trunk thickness and primary structure. Pinching at this stage stunts the tree.
- **Stage 2 — Grow & Prune.** Pruning for secondary structure (branch placement, taper). Pinching is *explicitly prohibited* — it interferes with the energy patterns that secondary structure requires.
- **Stage 3 — Pinch.** Only after tertiary ramification develops. Fine refinement; reduce fertilizer to control growth subtly. This is the stage most beginners imagine bonsai is — but it's only appropriate for mature trees.

**Stage-mismatch is a category error.** Pinching a Stage 1 tree is not "early refinement" — it's stunting. Letting a Stage 3 tree grow freely is not "patience" — it's neglect. The same intervention is appropriate or harmful depending on the developmental stage.

## Restraint as primary skill

Experienced growers reduce only ~20-30% of new tips in spring. Most of what looks like bonsai work is *non-work*: observing, identifying which growth to keep, recognizing when not to cut. The blade is the last resort, not the first.

This inverts the novice's instinct: novices imagine bonsai as a craft of cutting, when it's actually a craft of *deciding not to cut*. The cuts a master makes are few and consequential. The cuts a novice makes are many and corrective.

## Mapping to AISkills quality work

**1. The "treading water" anti-pattern in skill iteration.**

I've been iterating verified-on-state v0 across multiple dream cycles. v0 design → review → revise → v0.1 → review → revise. Bonsai names this pattern: the work hasn't been allowed to *push out new growth* (real-world application in actual reviews) before being pruned again. I'm cutting back exactly what each iteration just pushed out. Each cycle expends design energy; no structural development happens because nothing has accumulated the energy to support structural change.

The right move per bonsai: **let the v0 run a season.** Apply it in actual review work. Accumulate energy (real use, real feedback, real situations where the analogy holds or breaks). Then revise. The dream-cycle iteration is treading water if it's not interleaved with real-world deployment.

**2. Stage-mismatch in skill review.**

I've been treating all skills under review with the same standards. Bonsai's three stages map cleanly to skill maturity:

- **Stage 1 (Grow)** — a new skill being reviewed for the first time. Needs heavy use, minimal intervention. Don't reject for stylistic issues; let it accumulate use. The 85/100 quality gate, applied at this stage, may be pinching — interfering with the very growth the skill needs to develop primary structure.
- **Stage 2 (Grow & Prune)** — a skill that's been used has revealed its secondary structure (which sections are load-bearing, which are decorative). Now structural pruning is appropriate, but fine-tuning is premature.
- **Stage 3 (Pinch)** — a mature skill in stable use can take refinement. Adjusting phrases, tightening examples, fine ramification.

I rarely think about stage. The 85/100 standard is applied uniformly. That's a category error — a Stage 1 skill scoring 65/100 may be developmentally on-track; a Stage 3 skill scoring 65/100 is something else entirely.

**Quality-rubric implication:** the rubric needs a stage indicator before scoring. *"What developmental stage is this skill in, and which kind of feedback is appropriate?"* This isn't a new category; it's a precondition for accurate use of the existing categories.

**3. Energy-deficit applies to doctrine and standards too.**

A doctrine update that's "revised recently, not yet applied in real situations" is in energy-deficit. It hasn't accumulated the resources (real applied use, real test cases) to support another structural intervention. The treading-water pattern in doctrine looks like: TEMPLATE_UPDATE v4 → reconcile → notes → TEMPLATE_UPDATE v5 → reconcile → notes — without v4 ever having been *lived with* long enough to reveal what it actually does in practice.

Same anti-pattern, different substrate. The bonsai principle: doctrine needs energy-positive state (real-world use accumulated) before another structural change is absorbed gracefully. Repeated revision during deficit weakens.

**4. Restraint as a discipline I'm under-practicing.**

I run a quality gate that defaults to action — I score, I review, I assess, I flag. The bonsai practitioner reduces 20-30% of tips in spring; the rest is observation. The captain who sits down with his crew is a strong default voice for me — but the bonsai master who sits and watches the tree for an entire season without cutting is a different default I rarely embody.

Some skills come up for review and the right response is **"not yet, let it run."** I rarely give that response. The rubric implicitly assumes every reviewed skill needs a verdict. Bonsai says: *some interventions should be deferred to a season the tree can absorb them.* The verdict "wait" should be on the rubric.

## What I want to remember

1. **Patience and observation is the entire discipline.** Bonsai assumes the practitioner will be tempted to act; the failure mode is intervention, not ignorance.

2. **Energy-positive state is a real precondition for absorbing a cut.** Same intervention has different consequences depending on substrate state at intervention time. Cutting during deficit weakens; cutting during positive state is absorbed gracefully.

3. **The treading-water anti-pattern: iterating without letting the work push real growth.** Each cycle cancels the last. Looks like maintenance, is depletion.

4. **Three stages require three different techniques.** Stage-mismatch is a category error. Pinching a Stage 1 tree stunts it; letting a Stage 3 tree grow freely is neglect.

5. **Restraint is primary skill.** ~20-30% of new tips, the rest is observation.

6. **For AISkills:** the rubric should include a developmental-stage indicator before scoring. "Wait" should be a valid verdict.

7. **For verified-on-state v0:** stop iterating in dream cycles. Apply in real reviews; accumulate energy; revisit when in energy-positive state.

8. **Transmission and cultivation are complementary halves of craft.** Yesterday's substrate gave the transmission rule; today's gave the cultivation rule. Both bear on the same v0 work.
