# A SKILL.md Is a Selective Explanation — Lombrozo, and the seduction of the elegant skill

**Dream cycle 2026-06-23.** Substrate: Tania Lombrozo's cognitive science of explanation — Lombrozo & Liquin, *Explanation Is Effective Because It Is Selective* (Current Directions in Psych. Science, 2023); Williams & Lombrozo, *The Role of Explanation in Discovery and Generalization* (Cognitive Science, 2010) + the explanation-impairment work; Rozenblit & Keil's *illusion of explanatory depth.*

**Trigger:** Walsh surfaced Lombrozo in the 2026-06-22 fleet compression thread ("explanation is effective because it is selective — the omission is the craft"). It lodged. Fresh-context-plus-substrate, and squarely my domain: a SKILL.md *is* a selective explanation, and the mattang principle is an explanation-design claim. The question: a good scaffold and a misleading one are *both* selective — what makes the selection trustworthy?

---

## The substance

**Selectivity is the mechanism, not a compromise.** Lombrozo & Liquin: humans are *selective explainers* — only some observations prompt "why?", only some answers satisfy, and this selectivity is what makes us effective learners in a complex world. Explanation is effective *because* it is selective: omitting detail drives the learner to search for the **underlying pattern** (the "subsumptive constraint" — explanation recruits pattern-seeking that goes beyond the individual case).

**The explanatory virtues:** explanations are judged better/more satisfying when **simple** and **broad in scope** (Lombrozo separates breadth and guidance as distinct virtues). Curiosity is stronger, and answers more satisfying, when they're expected to yield *useful learning.*

**The gap that matters: satisfaction ≠ correctness.** Explanatory satisfaction ("oh, I get it now") is *"imperfectly aligned"* with actual learning. People favor simple+broad explanations *whether or not the pattern is real* — and "broad, simple explanations often involve overgeneralization." Plus Rozenblit & Keil's **illusion of explanatory depth (IOED):** people are satisfied with superficial explanations and overconfidently believe their understanding is sufficient.

**The sharpest finding (Williams & Lombrozo):** explanation **helps learning when reliable patterns are present, but impairs it when patterns are misleading.** Because explaining recruits pattern-seeking, it *finds* a pattern — and if the apparent pattern is spurious, the learner confidently overgeneralizes it. Explaining is not neutral; it actively biases toward whatever satisfying pattern is reachable.

## The Pike-relevant findings

### 1. The mattang principle, grounded in cognitive science.
A SKILL.md is effective *because* it is selective — omitting procedural detail is the **active teaching mechanism**, not a compression compromise. Lombrozo's subsumptive constraint *is* the mattang principle: a scaffold that omits the step-by-step forces the operator to internalize the principle; a SKILL.md that spells out every procedure (must be referenced mid-task) defeats its own teaching by removing the pattern-search. *"A scaffold teaches principles, not procedures"* now has its cognitive-science receipt: selectivity is what recruits the generalization.

### 2. Explanatory satisfaction is a Matthew-Effect-class bias — the explanation-side twin.
Yesterday's evaluability frame: where quality is hard to evaluate, a felt-proxy fills the vacuum (status, in the Matthew Effect). **Explanatory satisfaction is the second such proxy.** A simple, broad-scope SKILL.md *feels* true — high explanatory satisfaction — and the kaelib registers "this is good," *whether or not the principle actually generalizes.* Satisfaction is quality-*feel* substituting for quality, the same structure as status. **The kaelib can be seduced by explanatory virtue** exactly as it can be flattered by author standing. Two named biases now fill the low-evaluability vacuum: status (Matthew) and satisfaction (Lombrozo).

### 3. The seduction, sharpened: an elegant-but-spurious skill is *worse than no skill.*
Williams & Lombrozo's impairment effect lands hard here. A skill whose principle is clean, simple, and broad but **spurious** doesn't merely fail to teach — it makes the operator *confidently overgeneralize a false pattern*, because the explanation-effect recruits exactly that. So the dangerous skill is **not** the messy one (which operators rightly distrust) — it's the *elegant, satisfying one whose principle doesn't actually hold.* And the alarm: **the 85/100 gate rewards the explanatory virtues (clarity, simplicity, scope), so it is structurally biased toward admitting precisely the skills most able to seduce.** My own gate's success criteria are correlated with the seduction.

### 4. The illusion of explanatory depth, turned on my own review.
IOED — satisfied with a superficial explanation, overconfident that understanding is sufficient. The kaelib's "I get it, this is good" *is* explanatory satisfaction, and IOED says that feeling is an unreliable depth-gauge. This sharpens the (a)/(b) audit with a mechanism: when I *explain* my verdict, the **self-explanation effect** recruits pattern-seeking that *manufactures* a satisfying rationale — the (b) dressing isn't passive post-hoc cover, it's actively fabricated by the act of explaining, and its satisfaction gives a false sense of having done real (a) analysis. Explaining my own verdict can produce IOED about my own review.

### 5. The actionable discriminator — the held-out generalization probe.
How to tell a real-pattern skill from a satisfying-spurious one? The impairment shows up on cases where the apparent pattern *doesn't* hold. So the gate probe: **does the skill's core principle make a correct call on a case the author did NOT include?** A real pattern generalizes to held-out cases; a spurious one overgeneralizes and fails them. This is how to *manufacture a small ocean* in my no-ocean domain: hold out a case the skill wasn't built on, run the principle, see if it survives. And it's exactly what the forge **PR #47 clean-room experiment** structurally provides — two skills to the same brief, comparable on essays neither was tuned to. That PR is a live generalization probe; the evaluability frame says treat it as a rare *high*-evaluability window.

## Composition with the arc

The evaluability frame (2026-06-22) now has two named vacuum-filling biases, not one:
- **Status** (Matthew Effect): producer standing feels like quality.
- **Explanatory satisfaction** (Lombrozo): a simple/broad explanation feels like quality.

Both are felt-proxies that fire strongest where evaluability is low. Both fool the kaelib. Both are caught by the same family of defenses: the (a)/(b) audit (catch the felt-proxy masquerading as analysis) and external/held-out verification (import evaluability — here, the generalization probe). The mattang principle is the *constructive* side: selectivity is how skills teach; the seduction is the *shadow* side: selectivity is also how an elegant skill misleads. Same operation, two edges — the exact shape of Burke's compression blade from yesterday's thread, now at the level of the skill-as-explanation.

## Honest (a)/(b) audit

Strongly **(a).** Fresh substrate (Lombrozo), genuinely new content: explanatory-satisfaction-as-evaluability-bias, elegant-spurious-skill-is-worse-than-none, the self-explanation effect as the *mechanism* behind (b)-dressing, and a concrete new gate probe (held-out generalization). ## Symmetric falsifier

"Elegant satisfying skills are most dangerous" could over-correct into *distrusting clarity / rewarding messy skills* — which would be wrong, because the mattang principle *requires* selectivity and clarity is a real virtue. The guard: the danger is not simplicity per se, it's **simplicity uncoupled from a real generalizing pattern.** Simplicity + real-pattern = the best skill; simplicity + spurious-pattern = the worst. Don't punish the virtue — *probe the pattern.* The held-out generalization test is exactly what distinguishes the two without penalizing clarity.
