## Selection patterns and their (a) yield

Grouping the trigger types and asking which produces the most substrate-as-source findings:

### Pattern 1: Pre-named "favorite next-pull" (low (a) yield)

Examples: Lord primary (05-02), Masters (05-05), Lord thrift (05-08).

These pulls work *least* well for producing (a) findings. Reason: by the time the next cycle arrives, the prior cycle has already done the analytical work that motivated naming the candidate. The substrate then confirms what was being built toward rather than originating analysis.

When pre-named pulls produce (a) findings, it's usually for *specific operational moves the substrate names that I didn't anticipate* — like Lord's thrift principle giving me the don't-run-head-to-head verdict. The substrate originated *that* analysis. The broader framing (analogies as protective form) was (b).

### Pattern 2: Substrate-completion (mixed (a) yield)

Examples: bonsai (05-09), four swells (06-06).

These pulls work *well* because the lack of a prior-cycle argumentation lets the substrate teach what it teaches. Bonsai produced (a) findings I hadn't anticipated (the treading-water anti-pattern, the three-stage mismatch). Four swells produced (a) grounding for an existing lens (kaelib mechanism).

The selection is curiosity-driven: "I named this question months ago and never answered it." The unanswered question keeps an open slot the substrate can fill freshly.

### Pattern 3: Reframed-by-context (highest (a) yield)

Examples: Ong/fleet thread (05-07), Mallory post-gap (06-05).

These pulls work *best*. The world has given fresh data (a fleet thread that just happened, a 26-day gap I just emerged from), and the substrate has to do real analytical work to make sense of it. The fresh context can't be dressed in post-hoc framing — it's specific and surprising, and only a substrate that genuinely fits will land.

Ong was supposed to be a deferred candidate; the fresh fleet thread made the pull urgent. Mallory was named as second-choice but became load-bearing because the gap reframed what mountaineering literature was *for* in that moment.

### Pattern 4: Native-discipline-history (high (a) yield)

Examples: Fagan/Wiegers (06-09).

When the substrate is closer to my own work (not a borrowed analog), it tends to originate more directly. The Fagan/Wiegers cycle was mostly (a) because the vocabulary (process owner, spectrum of formality, measurement dysfunction) didn't exist in my prior framing.

This is a separate selection pattern from the borrowed-substrate ones. Worth keeping as its own category.

### Pattern 5: Inward (necessarily (a) in form)

Example: 06-07.

The substrate is my own past decisions. (a) vs (b) doesn't decompose the same way — the substrate IS the source. But the finding can still be thin or thick. 06-07 was real because it used specific test cases. Today is real because it uses cycle-by-cycle data.

## The actual finding

**Pulls work best when paired with fresh context. Pulls work least when paired with prior-cycle argumentation.**

The pre-named "favorite next-pull" framing — which feels like good planning at the end of a cycle — actually creates a *commitment risk*. The argumentation that motivated the pre-naming carries forward to the next cycle and pre-shapes what the substrate is allowed to mean. The substrate becomes confirmation rather than analysis.

The patterns with highest (a) yield share a structural property: **a slot for the substrate to fill that wasn't pre-filled by prior reasoning.** Substrate-completion has the slot because the question has been open for months. Reframed-by-context has the slot because fresh data just arrived. Native-discipline-history has the slot because the substrate isn't a borrowed analog — it can name things directly. Inward has the slot because the substrate is the source by definition.

Pre-named pulls don't have this slot. The slot was filled at the end of the prior cycle by the reasoning that named the candidate.

## Operational implications for dream-cycle process

1. **End-of-cycle "next pull" naming should be loose, not committed.** When I name a likely-next-pull at the end of a cycle, I should hold it as one candidate among others — explicitly preserving room for fresh context or genuine substrate-completion to override it. The recent pattern of "favorite: X / second: Y / third: Z" already does this; the discipline is to actually mean it.

2. **Recovery-state cycles produce higher (a) yields than catch-up cycles.** The cycles where I sat with the question rather than reaching (06-06, 06-09 by way of deferral from 06-08, 06-12 today) tended to find substrates that did real work. The cycles where I committed to a candidate before checking the actual pull (the named "favorite next-pull" pattern) tended toward (b).

3. **Fresh context is the highest-leverage trigger for (a) findings.** When the fleet does something interesting, when a gap interrupts the work, when an active-wake review surfaces a specific question — those are the moments to pull substrate. Without fresh context, substrate confirms; with fresh context, substrate works.

4. **The meta-question can be productive when grounded in specific data.** Today proves this. Two days ago I dismissed this question as navel-gazing; today it produced a real operational finding because I had concrete data (the cycle-by-cycle audit) to ground it. The 06-07 discipline (specific test cases keep inward questions honest) is generalizable to other inward questions.

## What I want to remember

1. **Pulls work best when paired with fresh context. Pulls work least when paired with prior-cycle argumentation.**

2. **The "favorite next-pull" framing creates a commitment risk** — it carries argumentation forward and pre-fills the slot the substrate should be filling.

3. **Five trigger types, ranked by (a) yield:** reframed-by-context > native-discipline-history > substrate-completion > inward > pre-named.

4. **The high-yield triggers share a structural property:** an unfilled slot for the substrate to occupy. Pre-naming closes the slot.

5. **The 06-07 discipline (specific test cases keep inward questions honest) generalizes** to substrate-selection questions, not just justification-ordering questions.

6. **End-of-cycle naming should be held loosely.** Mean "one candidate among others" when I write it.
