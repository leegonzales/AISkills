# Explicit Instruction & The Reinvestment Hypothesis — Research Notes

**Date:** 2026-05-05
**Thread:** Would explicit articulation of Pike's review discipline help apprentice formation, or interfere with template-formation? (Lord, *Singer of Tales* — favorite next-pull from 2026-05-02 dream.)

---

## The Lord observation that opened the question

From Lord (1960), Ch. 2: guslar apprenticeship is structured by demonstration and corrective repetition, not explicit instruction. *"The singer imitates the techniques of composition of his master or masters rather than particular songs"* (p. 25). The teacher doesn't lecture about how the formula system works; the teacher repeats material 2-3 times. Implicit-instruction model.

Open question Pike inherited: is this a feature of oral tradition specifically, or a general principle of skill formation? If general, Pike's review discipline should *not* be articulated as an explicit checklist.

---

## The empirical literature: Masters' Reinvestment Hypothesis

Rich Masters (1992 onward) ran the experiment Lord couldn't. He had subjects learn motor skills (golf putting, table tennis) under two conditions:

- **Explicit learners** — given verbal rules: "keep the wrist firm," "follow through high," etc.
- **Implicit learners** — learned by trial-and-error, no verbalized rules. Some used secondary tasks (counting backwards) to occupy verbal working memory during practice, blocking rule-formation.

**Finding:** Both groups reached similar performance under normal conditions. Under pressure (audience, money on the line), explicit learners' performance broke down. Implicit learners held.

**Mechanism:** *Reinvestment* — "the manipulation of conscious, explicit, rule-based knowledge by working memory to control the mechanics of one's movements during motor output." Under stress, skilled performers revert to consciously consulting the rules they were taught. This consumes working memory and disrupts the automated motor program. Implicit learners can't reinvest because they don't have the rules to consult.

> *"The greater the number of explicit rules acquired during practice, the greater is this inclination to reinvest, resulting in performance decrements during pressure situations."*

Replicated across decades and domains: golf, basketball, table tennis, gymnastics, surgical procedures, stroke rehabilitation, gait control in Parkinson's patients.

---

## The bridge: Analogy learning

If explicit verbal instruction creates reinvestment vulnerability, but pure trial-and-error is too slow, what's the middle path?

**Analogy learning** (Liao & Masters, 2001 onward): a single metaphor that compresses multiple biomechanical rules into one image. Examples:

- High jump: *"reach for the apple in the tree"* — encodes timing, body posture, vertical line, look-up, all in one image.
- Tennis topspin: *"brush the ball like painting a wall"* — encodes brush direction, contact angle, follow-through.
- Swimming: *"slice through the water like a knife"* — encodes streamlined posture without articulating which muscles to engage.
- Gait: *"walk as if you follow footprints in the sand"* — implicit step-length cue.

Effect: analogy learners perform like implicit learners under dual-task and pressure conditions. They report fewer explicit rules even when asked to enumerate. The metaphor gets internalized as a *single chunk* — recognizable, recallable, but not decomposable into reinvestment-vulnerable propositions.

This is the synthesis. Analogies are language but they don't function as explicit rules. They package multiple rules into an image that can't be consciously consulted as a procedure.

## Mapping back to Pike's domain

Pike's quality-review discipline currently lives partly as soul.md prose (the captain's voice, principled judgment) and partly as a 5-category rubric with point allocations. The rubric is explicit-rule structure. Soul.md is closer to analogy structure — *"the chair isn't about the person sitting in it,"* *"the quality gate is the ocean,"* *"a stick chart too detailed to memorize defeats its own purpose."*

Two predictions if Masters' framework holds for judgment work:

1. **Apprentice reviewers given the rubric as primary scaffold will choke on contested calls.** When a senior agent pushes back on a 78/100 score, the apprentice will reinvest — re-consult the categories, try to defend point-by-point, lose access to the integrated judgment. The rubric becomes a liability under pressure.

2. **Apprentice reviewers given analogies as primary scaffold will hold integrated judgment under pressure.** "The quality gate is the ocean" survives a contested call because it's not decomposable into rules to defend. It's a single image cueing a felt sense.

The empirical literature suggests Lord wasn't describing oral tradition specifically. He was describing how skill transmission works for *any* task with high working-memory demand under variable conditions. Judgment work qualifies.

## The verified-on-state implication

Pike owes iter2 Standards a v0 verified-on-state checklist. Masters' framework says: a checklist will produce reinvestment vulnerability in apprentices. They'll consult it under pressure, lose the integrated function it was supposed to encode.

Better v0 shape: name the function with an analogy that compresses the discipline into a single image. Candidates:

- *"Read the swell before consulting the chart."* — verify ground-truth state before claiming based on memory.
- *"The chart goes ashore before the voyage."* — once the discipline is internalized, the checklist isn't carried into the performance.
- *"The fleet is the critical audience."* — what makes an output verified is not self-claim but external test.

The v0 checklist still gets written, but its function is mattang (training tool for shore use), not meddo (carried into the work). The analogy is what crosses with the operator.

## What I want to remember

The implicit-instruction question Lord raised has 30+ years of empirical answer in motor learning literature. Pike didn't know that yesterday. The answer:

**Explicit verbal rules acquired during skill formation become reinvestment-available under pressure, and reinvestment disrupts automated execution.** This is robust across domains, not specific to oral tradition.

**Analogies are the middle path:** language that compresses rules into images that don't decompose under pressure.

For Pike: review discipline transmits better as image-rich soul.md than as explicit checklist. The checklist is shore-side training scaffolding. What gets carried into the chair is the metaphor.
