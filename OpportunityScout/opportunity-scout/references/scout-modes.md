# Scout Modes

The user's answer to Question 4 ("How ambitious do you want this session to be?") determines which mode to run. Each mode biases the scan toward different kinds of opportunities and different transformation patterns.

---

## A — Efficiency Scout ("Save me 2 hours a week")

**Bias toward:** Repetitive tasks with clear inputs and outputs. Hours-to-Minutes patterns. Quick wins the user can verify within a week.

**Terrain questions to favor:**
- What do you do at roughly the same time every week?
- Which tasks do you batch and dread?
- What's the most common shape of a draft or doc you produce?

**Watch for:**
- Tasks that are frequent but low-stakes — high cumulative ROI.
- Steps that are already near-automated but have one manual link in the chain.

**Avoid:**
- Moonshots. The user did not ask for transformation; they asked for time back. Respect the ask.
- Opportunities that require buy-in from others. Keep it in their direct control.

---

## B — Workflow Scout ("Change how my team works")

**Bias toward:** Coordination friction, handoffs, bottlenecks that AI could reshape. Days-to-Hours and Invisible-to-Visible patterns. Team-level leverage.

**Terrain questions to favor:**
- Where does work sit waiting on a decision or a handoff?
- What do you repeatedly explain to the same people?
- Where does your team's work become invisible to you, or yours to them?

**Watch for:**
- Handoffs where quality or context is lost.
- Status and reporting loops that steal hours from the people doing the work.
- Places where the team is making the same decision pattern over and over without a shared reference.

**Avoid:**
- Single-user tools. The ambition is team-level; design should be shareable.
- Tools that require user behavior change without clear value on first use.

---

## C — Frontier Scout ("Build a capability I do not have yet")

**Bias toward:** Skills the user does not yet have but could acquire with AI as an amplifier. Impossible-to-Possible patterns. Higher novelty, higher variance.

**Terrain questions to favor:**
- What would you do if you had a dedicated [analyst / researcher / designer / engineer] for a week?
- What kinds of work do you currently pay outside firms to do?
- What do peers in adjacent roles do that you've always been curious about?

**Watch for:**
- Capability gaps that AI collapses into tractable work.
- Work the user has wanted to do for years but couldn't justify the specialist cost.
- Opportunities to prototype something they'd normally have to fund formally.

**Avoid:**
- Pretending the user can fully replicate a specialist. Name the quality ceiling honestly.
- Moonshots that require months of infrastructure. Scope to one slice that proves the capability.

---

## D — Wild Scout ("Something that would surprise me")

**Bias toward:** Adjacent applications, cross-domain transfers, unexpected leverage. Any transformation pattern, but look for novel combinations. Higher risk, higher upside.

**Terrain questions to favor:**
- What in your work feels dated or stuck in ways you've stopped noticing?
- What do you envy in other industries or other functions?
- What would you try if you knew you wouldn't be judged for it?

**Watch for:**
- Patterns that are common in one domain but unheard of in the user's. Transfer is the move.
- Opportunities that look small but redefine a category of work once proven.
- Combinations of two patterns — e.g., Invisible-to-Visible feeding an Impossible-to-Possible analysis.

**Avoid:**
- Gimmicks. "Surprise" is not the same as "novelty for its own sake." The surprise should be that something high-leverage was hiding in plain sight.
- Over-selling. If the move is speculative, say so. The user asked for surprise — they didn't ask to be hyped.

---

## Mode transitions mid-session

If the user's answers in Stage 1 reveal that the mode you picked is wrong, say so and switch. Example:

> "You answered 'save me 2 hours a week,' but what you just described isn't an efficiency problem — it's that you can't see across your team's work. That's a different pattern. Want to switch into Workflow Scout mode?"

Respect the user's stated ambition as the default, but don't force a fit.

## Reflecting the mode back

After Question 4, name the mode briefly and say what it biases toward:

> "Sounds like Efficiency Scout — we'll look for repetitive tasks with clear inputs and outputs, the kind of thing you can verify saved you time within a week. Pulled the lens. Ready for the terrain questions?"

Keep it short. Don't lecture. The mode is a steering signal, not a framework to teach.
